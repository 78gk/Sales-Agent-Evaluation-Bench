# From Quantity to Quality: Boosting LLM Performance with Self-Guided Data Selection for Instruction Tuning

**Authors:** Ming Liu, Raphael Tang, Xinyu Li, Shengbin Yao, Jiapeng Wu, Fuli Luo, Xu Sun
**Venue:** COLM 2024 (Conference on Language Modeling)
**Year:** 2024

---

## Core Contribution

- Proposes SELF-FILTER, a data-selection method for instruction-tuning datasets: given a
  large pool of candidate instruction-response pairs, use the model being trained to score
  each example by its own uncertainty (perplexity on the response given the instruction),
  then discard examples where the model has either near-zero uncertainty (too easy — already
  learned) or near-maximum uncertainty (too hard — unlearnable at current capability).
- Demonstrates that selecting the 5–20% of examples in the "productive learning zone" (the
  middle of the perplexity distribution) consistently outperforms training on the full set,
  often by 5–15 percentage points on downstream tasks.
- Extends the LIMA quality-over-quantity principle with a *dynamic* criterion: quality is
  not a fixed attribute of the example, it is a function of what the model currently knows.
  An example that is "productive" at step 1 of training may be "too easy" at step 1,000.
- Requires no external judge model, no human annotation, and no additional API calls — only
  the model's own forward pass over each candidate.

---

## How It Informs Tenacious-Bench

The Liu et al. perplexity-based curriculum directly informs the Day 4 augmentation strategy
for Tenacious-Bench. The 75 training tasks will be augmented to ~1,500 pairs via paraphrase
rotation (LIMA-style). SELF-FILTER's insight suggests we should not train on all 1,500
equally — we should select the subset where the base Qwen 3.5 model has the highest
perplexity on the correct phrasing-tier response (the hard cases where the model most needs
training signal) and skip the easy cases where the base model already predicts the correct
tier. This is implemented in `training/prepare_sft_data.py` as the LIMA-quality filter
step: pairs where the evaluator's score under the base model already exceeds 0.80 are
downsampled to reduce their weight in the training batch.

---

## Design Choice Under Examination

**Section 3.2 ("Self-Guided Selection Criterion"):** The authors use *response* perplexity
— the model's uncertainty on the ground-truth response given the instruction — as the
quality signal. The assumption is that high-perplexity examples are uniformly "harder" and
therefore more informative. The selection criterion is model-agnostic: the same perplexity
threshold applies regardless of which *type* of difficulty is driving the uncertainty.

---

## My Disagreement

Perplexity conflates two categorically different sources of difficulty that require different
training interventions. For Tenacious-Bench, uncertainty can arise from:

1. **Tier-decision uncertainty:** The model is unsure whether conf=0.49 requires `hypothesis`
   or `inquiry`. This is the failure mode we are training against — high perplexity here
   is productive signal.

2. **Lexical uncertainty:** The model is unsure whether the response should say "funding
   round" or "capital event" or "investment." This is stylistic variation with no bearing
   on phrasing-tier correctness — high perplexity here is noise.

A single perplexity threshold over the full response will select examples from both
categories indiscriminately. In our scoring architecture, the `phrasing_tier` field carries
0.60 weight and is checked by exact string match — so a response with correct tier label
but uncertain lexical choices will have high perplexity (unpredictable word choice) despite
being a correct and high-value training example.

In Week 10 Trace sim_f50f1801 (task 105, reward=0.0), the agent produced fluent, low-
perplexity text that failed phrasing-tier correctness: the language was predictable given
the model's prior (assertive sells confidently), but wrong given the confidence gate. A
SELF-FILTER selection on that example would discard it as "too easy" (low perplexity),
when it is precisely the kind of example where training signal is most needed — the model
is confidently wrong. The Liu et al. framework provides no mechanism for selecting
examples where the model is *certain but incorrect*, only examples where it is *uncertain*.

The correct curriculum for a confidence-gate task is not perplexity-over-full-response but
*error-rate-on-the-target-dimension* (phrasing_tier correctness under the base model) —
which our `scoring_evaluator.py` can compute directly. This is a structurally different
signal that the paper does not consider.
